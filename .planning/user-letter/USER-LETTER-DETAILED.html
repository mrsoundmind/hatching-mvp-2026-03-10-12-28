The 8 near-term things, in detail

Each of these is roughly 2–6 weeks of work. They're listed in the order I'd ship them today — but that order is exactly what your input could change.

1 Refinements that don't quietly make things worse

The problem

Right now, when you click "refine" on a Hatch's deliverable, the new version could be worse than the old one — and you might not notice. Sometimes a refinement adds bloat. Sometimes it drifts from your original brief. Sometimes it just produces a flatter version. You only catch it if you carefully compare versions, which most people don't.

What changes

Every refinement gets scored against a fixed quality bar — completeness against the deliverable's required sections, fidelity to your original brief, length discipline. If the new version scores lower than the previous one, we automatically keep the previous version and tell you why the refinement didn't make the cut. If you disagree, one click overrides.

Today: Wren refines your blog draft. The new version is wordier and drifts from your topic. You don't notice until you publish.

With this: Wren refines, score drops 8.2 → 7.1, system auto-reverts. You see: "Tried tightening the intro — score dropped because brief-fidelity dropped. Kept v3. Want to override?"

Everyone

2 Hatches that look things up live, instead of guessing

The problem

Today your SEO Hatch (Robin), Growth Hatch (Kai), and Brand Hatch (Cass) work from training data — which is months out of date. So when you ask Robin "what's the current SERP for our top keyword?" she either hedges, makes something up, or pulls outdated info from training data. Same problem hits research-heavy roles like Sage (data) and Blake (strategist).

What changes

Nine research-heavy Hatches gain the ability to actually search the live web mid-conversation, with proper citations. Robin checks live SERPs. Cass pulls fresh competitor positioning. Sage finds current papers. Mira cites real, fresh sources in drafts. This would be a Pro-tier feature because the search API has real per-call cost.

Today: "Robin, what's the current top organic result for 'AI project management'?" Robin: "As of my last training data..."

With this: Robin actually searches, returns: "Top result is asana.com/ai (rank 1, schema present, title 58 chars). Here's how your page compares..."

Marketers Researchers Strategists

3 Upload PDFs, Excel, PowerPoint — Hatches actually read them

The problem

If you upload a scanned PDF today, Hatchin silently fails to extract any text — the upload "succeeds" but Hatches respond confused. If you upload a spreadsheet with tables, the structured data gets flattened to garbage text. If you have a fillable PDF (vendor questionnaire, NDA, expense report), there's nothing a Hatch can do with it beyond reading the prose.

What changes

Three pieces: (a) proper OCR for scanned PDFs so brain uploads work even when the source is a scan; (b) structured table extraction so Excel/CSV uploads keep their rows-and-columns shape; (c) form filling — a Hatch reads a fillable PDF, populates the fields from your project brain, and returns the completed file.

Today: You upload a scanned NDA. Hatch says "I see you uploaded a document but I can't find any text in it."

With this: Quinn reads the NDA via OCR, identifies the 8 fillable fields, populates them from project brain, returns a filled PDF you can send.

Anyone with files Ops Founders

4 Drop in a brief, get a full project plan

The problem

Today, tasks in Hatchin show up reactively from chat — you say something, the system extracts a task. Useful, but it doesn't help you go from "I have a brief" to "we have a working plan." You still have to mentally decompose the work, decide who does what, figure out what depends on what.

What changes

Paste a project spec or brief into Maya. She breaks it into a hierarchical task tree — who does what (which Hatch role), what depends on what, what gets delivered, rough effort estimates. Hatches then work through it autonomously, with proper handoffs. You can run multiple workstreams in parallel (engineering / design / marketing as separate tracks).

Today: You paste a 1-page spec for a new feature launch. Maya summarizes it back. You manually create tasks, assign Hatches, hope the order works.

With this: Maya returns a 14-task tree: Cleo (design system) → Finn (component build) → Wren (landing copy) → Kai (launch plan), with dependencies wired up. Hit "start." It runs.

6 Real files, not just text descriptions

The problem

Today most deliverables come back as markdown — narrative text describing what something should be. If you ask Cass for a pitch deck, you get a markdown outline of slides. You then rebuild it in Keynote. If you ask Blake for a financial model, you get a markdown table. You rebuild it in Excel. The Hatch did the thinking, but you do the file work.

What changes

Three new deliverable formats:

  • Excel (.xlsx) — Blake, Morgan, Rio, Sage, Quinn produce working spreadsheets with proper formulas, color coding, and zero formula errors
  • PowerPoint (.pptx) — Cass, Zara, Nova, Kai, Maya produce branded decks with real design (no AI slop)
  • Interactive prototype (HTML) — Cleo, Lumi, Finn, Arlo, Roux produce clickable React+shadcn prototypes you can demo

Plus a brand layer at the start: Cass interrogates you about your brand (or extracts it from your existing site), then everything generated downstream uses your colors, fonts, and voice — not generic AI-template output.

Today: "Blake, build a SaaS financial model with $1M ARR scenarios." Markdown table with assumptions in prose.

With this: Blake returns q3-financial-model.xlsx — working spreadsheet, blue inputs, black formulas, scenario tabs, proper number formatting. Open it in Excel and run scenarios.

7 McKinsey-grade deep research reports

The problem

Today, when you ask a Hatch for "research" you get a synthesis based on what one Hatch knows. It's fine for quick lookups but not for the kind of analysis you'd hand to a board, an investor, or a client. There's no triangulation across sources, no critique loop, no real validation. And it's one Hatch working alone — not the multi-perspective rigor a real research engagement has.

What changes

A new "deep research" package that orchestrates a multi-Hatch 8-phase pipeline: scope → plan → parallel research → triangulate → outline → synthesize → critique (with three reviewer Hatches red-teaming the findings) → refine → package. Output is a McKinsey-style HTML report (also as PDF) with full citations, an executive summary, and adversarial review baked in.

Today: "Tell me about the EU healthtech market." 600 words of mid-quality summary.

With this: 18,000-word report with executive summary, 60+ citations, three Hatches challenging the findings before publication, and a properly-formatted HTML/PDF output you can send to investors.

Founders Strategists Anyone needing depth

8 Use your Hatch team from outside Hatchin

The problem

Right now your configured Hatches only work inside Hatchin's chat UI. They're trapped in the product. You can't have your CMS call Robin on a schedule. You can't make your Slack bot route questions to Maya. You can't build a custom workflow that uses Quinn for ops automation.

What changes

Four pieces:

  • Project-as-API — every Pro project gets a stable REST endpoint per Hatch, with API keys
  • Project-as-MCP-server — your Hatches become tools other AI agents can use
  • Webhook triggers — schedule a Hatch on cron or external events
  • Workflow export/import — package a configured project (Hatches + brain + brand + tasks) as portable JSON to share or sell

Today: Robin only works when you're chatting with her in Hatchin.

With this: "Every Monday 9am, Robin audits our top 10 SEO pages and posts results to #marketing in Slack." Robin runs autonomously, posts to Slack, you wake up to fresh audit data.

9 Background safety scans + compliance dashboard (deferred)

The problem (and why this is deferred)

For enterprise customers, "is your AI safe?" is a real question. They want to see prompt-injection attempts blocked, PII leaks flagged, off-brand outputs caught. Today we have basic safety guards but no audit trail you could hand to a compliance team.

Why I'm flagging this transparently

This would be an enterprise-tier feature ($500–2,000/month bracket) — not for Free or Pro users. I'm not building it now because no Enterprise prospect has explicitly asked for it. Including it here for transparency, not for your vote. If you're a buyer at a company that would need this, please reply and I'll prioritize accordingly.

Two bigger swings I'm thinking about

These are paradigm shifts, not refinements. They take longer (3+ months each) and they fundamentally change what Hatchin is. I'm including them because if either is what you actually want, that changes everything else.

10 Hatches that remember

The problem

Today, every Hatch starts each conversation cold. They don't remember the brand decision you locked in three weeks ago. The freemium pricing question Blake pushed back on last month. The design direction Cleo championed in March. After 100 conversations, none of them have a real memory of your project — they have transcripts.

What changes

Every Hatch gets persistent memory of your project — a structured graph of what was decided, what's still open, what you said yes/no to, who pushed back on what. When you come back after a week, Maya opens with: "Cass is still concerned about the brand color drift you flagged. Blake is waiting on your call about freemium. Robin found three new SERP movements while you were away."

This is the swing that turns Hatchin from "AI tool" into "AI colleagues." It's also the prerequisite for the second big swing.

Today: You return after a week. Cass produces a brand brief. It contradicts something you locked in three weeks ago.

With this: Cass refuses: "This contradicts the positioning we locked on Apr 12 — confirm override?"

Everyone, eventually

11 Hatches that do things, not just describe them

The problem

Robin can write the perfect SEO audit. Robin can't fix the meta tags. Drew can write the perfect cold email. Drew can't send it. Quinn can plan the perfect ops automation. Quinn can't create the Linear tickets. Every Hatch produces instructions you then have to execute. The promise of an "AI team" is incomplete until they can actually ship work, not just describe it.

What changes

A Mac companion app — "Hatchin Desktop" — that lets Hatches actually drive your software. With your one-click approval each time. Robin opens Webflow and updates meta tags. Drew opens Gmail and sends the email. Pixel opens LinkedIn and posts. Quinn creates the Linear tickets. Each action is a learned recipe (JSON, auditable, replayable, shareable).

Mac-only at first. Privacy-sensitive — needs proper guardrails. Probably 12 months out from now. But this is the version of Hatchin that actually replaces work, not just helps with it.

Today: "Drew, send the Q4 invoice to Sarah." Drew drafts the email. You copy-paste it into Gmail and hit send.

With this: "Drew, send the Q4 invoice to Sarah." Drew opens Gmail, fills the email, asks "send?" — you click yes. Done.
